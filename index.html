01

Entry, Exit & Long-Run Profitability

Why economic profits are hard to keep, what happens when competitors can freely enter and exit, and how some firms stay profitable anyway.
02
Part I

Revenue, Costs, and Economic Profits

03
Revenue

Price equals average revenue for any seller.

Revenue is the money a firm receives from selling its output. Total revenue equals price times quantity sold.

For any firm, the price it charges is also its average revenue per unit sold. That is true whether the firm is a price-taker in a perfectly competitive market or a price-setter with market power.

Revenue = Price x Quantity

04

Economic profit is narrower than accounting profit.

Accounting Profit

Revenue minus explicit costs only.

Explicit costs are out-of-pocket payments: wages, rent, materials, utilities.

This is what appears on a firm's income statement.

Economic Profit

Revenue minus all costs, including opportunity costs.

Opportunity cost: the value of the next best alternative you gave up.

A firm earning zero economic profit is still covering all its costs, including the owner's time and capital.

05
Costs

Some costs are fixed. Others vary with output.

Fixed costs do not change as the firm produces more or less. Rent on a building, software licenses, insurance premiums. In the short run these are unavoidable.

Variable costs rise with output. Labor hours, raw materials, energy consumed in production. To make more you spend more.

Total cost = Fixed costs + Variable costs. Average cost spreads total cost across each unit produced.

06
Average Cost

Average cost tells you what each unit costs to produce.

AC = TC / Q
As output rises, fixed costs get spread over more units, so the fixed-cost component of average cost falls. Variable cost per unit tends to rise as production strains capacity. The combination produces the U-shaped average cost curve central to this chapter.
AC = Fixed cost / Q + Variable cost / Q
07

The Average Cost Curve Is U-Shaped

Fixed costs per unit fall as output rises. Variable costs per unit rise with output. Their sum, average cost, forms a U. The minimum point is the most efficient scale of production.
08
Profit margin

The profit margin is the gap between price and average cost.

Profit margin = Price - Average cost
If price exceeds average cost, each unit sold earns a positive margin. If price is below average cost, every unit sold generates a loss. The margin is the per-unit story. Total economic profit multiplies the margin by the quantity sold.
Economic profit = (Price - AC) x Quantity
09

Profit Margin: Price Above Average Cost

The firm's demand curve is downward sloping. It sets quantity where profit is maximized. At that quantity, price exceeds average cost: the vertical gap is the profit margin per unit.
10

Three Outcomes: Profit, Loss, or Zero Profit

Whether a firm earns positive, negative, or zero economic profit depends entirely on where its demand curve intersects (or fails to intersect) its average cost curve.
Positive profit: demand crosses above AC
Zero profit: demand just touches AC at its minimum
11
Part II

Free Entry and Exit in the Long Run

12
Market interdependence

Your market position depends on who else is competing.

In imperfectly competitive markets, firms sell differentiated products. Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve because some customers prefer its particular product, brand, or location.

But those customers also have alternatives. When rivals enter the market, they pull customers away. Your firm's demand curve shifts left and becomes flatter, meaning customers become more price-sensitive because they have more substitutes.

When rivals exit, the reverse happens: your demand curve shifts right and steepens.

13

New Entry: Demand Shifts Left and Flattens

When new firms enter your market, customers have more alternatives. Your firm loses market share (leftward shift) and your remaining customers become more price-sensitive (curve flattens, becomes more elastic).
Before entry: steeper demand
After entry: flatter, shifted left
14

Exit: Demand Shifts Right and Steepens

When rivals leave the market, their customers need somewhere else to go. Your firm gains market share (rightward shift) and your customers become less price-sensitive because they have fewer alternatives (curve steepens).
Before exit: flatter demand
After exit: steeper, shifted right
15
The long-run mechanism

Economic profits tend toward zero through entry and exit.

Positive economic profits
→
New rivals enter
→
Market share falls, market power falls
→
Quantity falls, Price falls
→
Profits decline
Positive profits attract entry until profits fall to zero
Negative economic profits
→
Some rivals exit
→
Market share rises, market power rises
→
Quantity rises, Price rises
→
Losses shrink
Losses trigger exit until losses shrink to zero
16
Free entry and exit

Free entry pushes economic profits to zero in the long run.

Free entry occurs when there are no significant obstacles preventing new businesses from entering an industry. No patents, no government licenses required, no prohibitively large startup costs.

When entry is free, positive economic profits are a signal that attracts new competitors. Those competitors keep entering until profits fall to zero.

Free exit ensures industries do not remain permanently unprofitable. Firms exit when they cannot cover their costs, and that exit continues until the remaining firms break even.

17

Free Entry Continues Until Profits Reach Zero

Short-run profits attract entrants. Each wave of entry shifts the firm's demand curve left and flattens it. Entry stops when demand just touches the minimum of average cost.
Short-run profits
New firms enter
More firms enter
Zero profits: long-run equilibrium
18

Free Exit Continues Until Losses Reach Zero

Short-run losses push firms out. Each wave of exit shifts remaining firms' demand curves right and steepens them. Exit stops when demand just touches the minimum of average cost.
Short-run losses
Incumbent firms exit
More firms exit
Zero losses: long-run equilibrium
19
Long-run equilibrium

In the long run, with free entry and exit, price equals average cost.

Price = Average Cost
Free entry pushes price down toward average cost. Free exit pushes price up toward average cost. The process ends when the two are equal, meaning economic profit is exactly zero. The firm covers all its costs, including opportunity costs, but earns nothing beyond that.
Economic profit = (Price - AC) x Q = 0
20

Three Cases: Entry, Exit, or Equilibrium

Where demand sits relative to average cost tells you what will happen next. Only when demand just touches AC at its minimum does the market rest.
Demand crosses above AC: positive profit, entry occurs
Demand never reaches AC: losses, exit occurs
Demand just touches AC: zero profit, equilibrium
21
Practice Question

As firms enter a market, their firm demand curves

  • A. flatten.
  • B. steepen.
  • C. remain the same.
  • D. can flatten, steepen, or remain the same.

A is correct. Entry increases the number of substitutes available to customers, making each firm's remaining demand more elastic (flatter curve).

22
Practice Question

In imperfect competition with free entry, the long-run relationship among MC, MR, Price, and AC is:

  • A. MC = MR < Price = AC
  • B. MC = MR = Price = AC
  • C. MC = AC < Price = MR
  • D. MC = MR = AC < Price

A is correct. The profit-maximizing condition MC = MR holds, but the downward-sloping demand means price exceeds MR. Free entry drives price down to average cost, giving zero profit.

23
Part III

Barriers to Entry

24
Barriers to entry

Ongoing profitability depends on keeping rivals out.

Barriers to entry are obstacles that make it difficult or costly for new firms to enter an industry. When barriers are high, incumbents can sustain positive economic profits in the long run because entry does not erode them away.

When barriers are low or absent, profits attract entry and the long-run equilibrium returns to zero economic profit.

Understanding barriers to entry is the key to understanding why some industries remain highly profitable for decades while others are brutally competitive.

25

Four strategies firms use to deter entry

1
Demand-side: create customer lock-in Switching costs, brand loyalty, and network effects make customers reluctant to leave.
2
Supply-side: develop unique cost advantages Learning by doing, scale economies, R&D, and supplier relationships that entrants cannot replicate.
3
Regulatory: mobilize government to limit entry Patents, licenses, and lobbying for regulations that create legal barriers.
4
Deterrence: convince rivals you will crush them Credible threats of price wars or aggressive retaliation discourage entry before it happens.
26
Demand-side strategies

Lock in customers so they are expensive to poach.

  • Switching costs make it costly for customers to leave. Proprietary formats, long-term contracts, and accumulated data all create friction.
  • Reputation and goodwill build trust that new entrants cannot credibly claim overnight.
  • Network effects mean your product becomes more valuable as more people use it. A messaging app with a billion users is hard to displace even with a technically superior alternative.
27
Supply-side strategies

Build cost advantages that entrants cannot match.

  • Learning by doing: production costs fall as cumulative experience grows. An incumbent with years of production history has lower costs than a new entrant starting from scratch.
  • Scale economies: large-scale production spreads fixed costs across more units, giving incumbents a per-unit cost advantage that small entrants cannot overcome.
  • R&D and patents: proprietary technology can legally exclude rivals for the life of the patent.
  • Exclusive supplier relationships: controlling access to key inputs denies rivals the raw materials they need.
28

Cost Advantages Generate Lasting Profits

Free entry drives price to the average cost of the marginal supplier (the least efficient firm willing to enter). If your costs are lower, you earn a profit margin even in long-run equilibrium.
29
Regulatory strategies

Government policy can create or eliminate barriers to entry.

  • Patents grant the legal right to be the only producer of an invention for a fixed period, typically 20 years.
  • Licensing requirements can make it difficult for new businesses to enter regulated industries like healthcare, finance, and utilities.
  • Compulsory licenses can erode patent protection and increase competition.
  • Lobbying is a regulatory strategy: incumbent firms push for rules that disadvantage potential entrants, raising the cost of entry for rivals without affecting their own operations.
30

Days to Start a Business Varies Dramatically by Country

World Bank data. Regulatory burden differs, shaping how easily new firms can enter local markets.
31
Discussion Question

IBM was once the largest and most prestigious technology company in the United States. How did the emergence of Microsoft (1975), Apple (1976), Dell (1984), and others lead to IBM's decline?

Consider: which barriers to entry did IBM rely on? Which strategies did new entrants use to overcome them? What does this tell you about the durability of market power?

32
Discussion Question

How does free entry in the long run eliminate desirable opportunities in areas of your own life?

Think about your choice of college major. If a particular field is known to produce high starting salaries, what happens over time as more students enter that major? What about job markets in growing cities?

The same mechanism operates in markets and in careers: profitable opportunities attract entrants until the advantage is competed away.

33
Discussion Question

How does each of the following protect the profitability of incumbent firms?

  • WhatsApp / messaging apps: the best reason to use it is because your friends do too. That is a network effect.
  • Mass production facilities: scale economies give incumbents lower per-unit costs than a small entrant could match.
  • Walmart's supplier contracts: Everyday Low Prices rest on Everyday Even Lower supplier prices, built over decades of scale.
  • Facebook's talent advantage: recruiting against a tech giant with deep pockets and a strong brand is hard for any startup.
34
Summary

What this chapter covers in three ideas.

  • Economic profit is the right measure. Accounting profit flatters by ignoring opportunity costs. Economic profit of zero means a business is viable, not failing.
  • Free entry and exit drive economic profits to zero. Positive profits attract entrants until the market reaches equilibrium at Price = Average Cost. Losses trigger exit until the same equilibrium is restored.
  • Barriers to entry are what protect lasting profitability. Customer lock-in, cost advantages, regulatory protection, and deterrence strategies are the tools incumbent firms use to defend their position.

Speaker Notes: Entry, Exit & Long-Run Profitability

Slides 1-2: Opening
Frame the chapter as answering a puzzle students already sense: why does starting a business in a profitable industry seem like a good idea, but the economics often disappoint? The answer is free entry. Every profitable opportunity becomes less profitable once others notice it. This is not pessimistic. It is the mechanism that allocates resources efficiently. Understanding it changes how students read business headlines.
Slides 3-6: Revenue, Costs, Economic Profit
The accounting vs. economic profit distinction trips students up every time. Use a concrete example: a student who quits a $40,000/year job to open a food truck that earns $45,000 in accounting profit. Is this a success? Economically, no, the opportunity cost of the foregone salary wipes out the gain. Ask: what would it take for this truck to earn positive economic profit?
Slide 7: U-Shaped Average Cost
Walk through the shape slowly. The left side of the U is dominated by fixed costs spreading over more units. The right side reflects the rising variable cost per unit as production strains capacity. The minimum of the U is the most efficient scale. In long-run equilibrium, free entry drives price to exactly this minimum.
Slides 8-10: Profit Margin
The profit margin diagram is the workhorse of this chapter. Price is the firm's demand curve (also its average revenue). The gap between price and average cost at the chosen quantity is the margin per unit. Total profit is that margin times quantity. When demand drops below the AC curve entirely, the firm cannot cover its costs at any output level: it is losing money regardless of what it produces.
Slides 12-15: Entry, Exit, and the Feedback Loop
The entry/exit mechanism is a feedback loop, not a one-time event. Walk through each step of the flow diagram carefully. Students often think market equilibrium means "nothing is changing." Emphasize that the long-run equilibrium is a state where there is no longer any pressure to change: entry is not profitable, exit is not necessary. That pressure ceases when profits hit zero.
Slides 17-18: Four-Panel Progressions
These four-panel diagrams are the heart of Wolfers' presentation. For entry: panel 1 shows positive profit (demand crosses above AC). Each subsequent panel shows demand shifting left and flattening as more firms enter. Panel 4 shows demand just tangent to the bottom of the AC curve: zero profit. For exit: the mirror image. Demand starts below AC (losses). Each exit wave shifts demand right and steepens it until tangency.
Slide 20: Three Cases
This is the diagnostic students need for any market structure problem. Ask: where does demand sit relative to average cost? If it crosses above AC, there are positive profits and entry will occur. If it never reaches AC, there are losses and exit will occur. If it just touches the minimum of AC, the market is in long-run equilibrium.
Slides 23-29: Barriers to Entry
This section bridges economics and business strategy. The four strategies (demand-side, supply-side, regulatory, deterrence) are the toolkit incumbents use to fight the long-run tendency toward zero profit. Network effects are particularly worth dwelling on: they are central to the economics of tech platforms and are frequently misunderstood. Students often say "Facebook is popular because it is good." Push back: it is popular partly because everyone else is already on it. That circularity is the network effect.
Slide 28: Cost Advantages Diagram
The key insight: free entry drives price to the average cost of the marginal supplier, meaning the least efficient firm still willing to enter. If your costs are lower than the marginal supplier's, you earn a profit margin in long-run equilibrium. This is why cost leadership is a durable competitive strategy and why Walmart spent decades building supply chain advantages that rivals found nearly impossible to replicate.
Slides 31-33: Discussion Questions
IBM: a useful case because students recognize the name and its decline, but often attribute it to poor management rather than the economics of entry. The real story is that IBM's barriers eroded: proprietary hardware became less important as software and standards (MS-DOS, the PC standard) shifted power elsewhere. Entrants faced lower costs because the inputs became commoditized. The majors discussion is personal and lands well: students can see that the salary premium for nursing or accounting shrinks over time as more people enter those fields.
