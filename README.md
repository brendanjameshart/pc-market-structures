# Entry, Exit & Long-Run Profitability

A slide-based lecture primer on market structures, competitive dynamics, and long-run profitability. Built for introductory and intermediate economics courses, and for general readers who want a grounded, diagram-driven treatment of how markets work and why economic profits are so hard to keep.

**Live presentation:** [brendanjameshart.github.io/pc-market-structures](https://brendanjameshart.github.io/pc-market-structures/)

---

## What this covers

The primer follows the arc of a competitive market from short-run profit to long-run equilibrium, then asks why some firms escape that pressure entirely.

**Part 1 — Economic profit and the cost curve**

- The difference between accounting profit and economic profit
- Why opportunity cost is the right benchmark for business decisions
- The U-shaped average cost curve: fixed costs, variable costs, and the minimum efficient scale
- How to read the profit margin — the gap between price and average cost

**Part 2 — Free entry and the end of profit**

- What happens when a market is earning positive economic profit
- How new entrants shift each incumbent firm's demand curve left
- The entry progression: four panels showing price falling from above AC to tangency
- Long-run equilibrium: zero economic profit, not zero accounting profit

**Part 3 — Free exit and the end of losses**

- What happens when a market is sustaining economic losses
- How exiting firms shift each remaining firm's demand curve right
- The exit progression: four panels showing price rising from below AC to tangency
- The same zero-profit equilibrium reached from below

**Part 4 — Three cases and what they mean**

- Markets currently earning profit, sustaining losses, or at break-even
- Why the long-run always converges to zero economic profit under free entry and exit
- What this implies for investment, wages, and resource allocation

**Part 5 — Barriers to entry and sustained profit**

- Why some firms stay profitable for decades despite competitive pressure
- Cost advantages: economies of scale, proprietary technology, learning curves
- Regulatory and legal barriers: patents, licenses, government protection
- Strategic barriers: network effects, switching costs, brand loyalty
- The business startup environment as a measure of how open a market really is
